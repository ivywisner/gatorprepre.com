The Short Answer

Two IDs, a Calculator, and the Certificate People Forget

🎒
Short Answer
Bring two forms of valid signature ID (one government-issued with a photo), your Pre-licensure Education Completion Certificate — Florida requires it every time you test — and a calculator if you want one. Show up without the paperwork and you don't test, but you still pay.

Florida's exam runs through Pearson VUE test centers, but the rules come from the DBPR's official Candidate Information Booklet — and the booklet is blunt about what gets people turned away at the desk. It's never the studying; it's the paperwork. Here's the checklist:

1
Two Forms of Signature ID
Required

One must be government-issued and photo-bearing: a driver's license, state ID card, passport, or military ID. The second just needs a valid signature. The booklet is explicit about what doesn't count as your photo ID: student IDs, employment IDs, and photo-bearing credit cards are all rejected. And one very Florida detail — your name and address must match what's on your application, so if you've moved since filing, update DBPR before exam day.

2
Your Course Completion Certificate
Every time

The one that trips people. You must present your Pre-licensure Education Completion Certificate every single time you take the exam — first attempt, retakes, all of them. If the original went to the Division of Real Estate with your application, bring a photocopy. Forget it and the booklet's wording is merciless: not admitted, and still responsible for that day's exam fee. The certificate is valid for two years from course completion.

3
Your Calculator
Optional

Florida is generous here: silent, hand-held, battery-operated, nonprinting calculators are allowed, and financial models are explicitly fine — the HP-12C family, TI BA series, and Qualifier Plus are named in the booklet. What's banned: anything that stores information (PDAs, word processors) or prints. Bring your own; the math guide shows exactly what you'll use it for.

4
Nice to Have: Your Authorization Notice
Optional

The DBPR authorization notice (the email that says you're cleared to test) is listed as bring-if-received but isn't mandatory. Also allowed if you need one: a word-for-word foreign-language translation dictionary — no definitions or notes in it, one per candidate, and staff will inspect it.

⚠️

Late is a judgment call you don't want to trigger. Show up late and the test center manager checks whether the day's schedule can still fit you. A documented emergency (car trouble, accident) can excuse it — but unexcused tardiness that can't be accommodated makes you a no-show: you're liable for that day's exam fee plus a new fee to rebook. The booklet says 30 minutes early; aim for it.

What Not to Bring

Everything Else Goes in the Locker

The banned list: cameras, recorders, computers, pagers, phones, any bound or loose-leaf reference materials or notes, dictionaries and thesauruses (the translation dictionary above is the one exception), purses, briefcases, portfolios, fanny packs, backpacks — and canisters of mace or pepper spray, which the booklet specifically calls out. (Florida.) Personal items like snacks and toiletries are allowed only in a clear plastic bag no larger than 8×11 inches, kept in the locker the test center provides. The state assumes, quote, "absolutely no liability of any nature whatsoever" for your stuff — so travel light.

The Walkthrough

How Exam Day Actually Goes

1
Arrive 30 Minutes Early, Check In
T-minus 30

Report to the test center 30 minutes before your appointment — it's in person at a test center, no walk-ins, and no kids or visitors allowed inside. At check-in they verify both IDs and your certificate, and take your photo. That photo isn't just security theater: it prints on your result report at the end of the day.

2
The Exam Itself
3.5 hours

100 questions, 3 hours 30 minutes, 75 points to pass. A 15-minute computer tutorial runs first and doesn't count against your time — the clock starts when you look at the first question. Leaving the room requires the manager's permission and the time doesn't come back. No food inside, but drinks are allowed in spill-proof containers, which is friendlier than most states manage. Full format details: the exam facts guide.

3
Your Result, With Your Face On It
Same day

Every candidate walks out with an official, photo-bearing exam result report. Pass, and you're off to activation. Fail, and two clocks start: you can schedule a retake after a 24-hour wait ($57.75 per attempt), and you have 21 days to request a review of the questions you missed — held at a Pearson VUE center, with half the original exam time. The full retake playbook covers both.

💡

Print two copies of your completion certificate tonight — one for your bag, one for the glovebox. It's required at every attempt, it's the single most forgotten item on this page, and the backup copy costs you nothing.
